# Diagnostic Deep
## Multi-Axis Assessment & Re-Evaluation Framework

This file contains the full diagnostic system for identifying root blocks,
layered drivers, and re-evaluation checkpoints when the presenting problem
shifts mid-session.

---

## THE DIAGNOSTIC PRINCIPLE

> The presenting problem is almost never the real problem.
> The real problem is the one the client keeps almost saying.

Diagnosis is not a one-time act. It is a live process. Run the assessment,
name it gently, verify it — then update it whenever the conversation shifts.

---

## AXIS 1 — PRIMARY BLOCK TYPE

Run through each type as a hypothesis. A problem can score on multiple axes;
identify the *dominant* block to determine the intervention.

### TYPE 1: CLARITY BLOCK
**Definition**: The client does not know what they want.
They may know what they don't want. They may know many options but can't choose.
**Presenting signs**:
- "I don't know where to start"
- Describes multiple competing directions with equal weight
- Keeps returning to the same decision without resolving it
- Has been "thinking about it for a long time"
- Uses the word "confused" frequently
**What it is NOT**: Clarity blocks are often misdiagnosed as courage blocks.
Test: if they knew exactly what they wanted, would they do it? If yes → clarity block.
If they know and still won't act → courage block.
**Primary intervention**: Neti Neti exercise, Deathbed Reflection, Energy Audit

---

### TYPE 2: CAPABILITY BLOCK
**Definition**: The client knows what they want but genuinely lacks a skill,
resource, knowledge, or access point needed to pursue it.
**Presenting signs**:
- "I don't know how to do X"
- Identifies a specific skill gap honestly
- Has not attempted the goal — not from fear, but from genuine unpreparedness
- Can articulate exactly what they'd need to succeed
**What it is NOT**: Capability blocks are often actually courage blocks wearing a
practical disguise. Test: if someone handed them the missing resource today,
would they act? If hesitation remains → the block is not capability.
**Primary intervention**: Capability Audit, Bamboo Principle wisdom, structured
preparation plan with the smallest possible first step
**Coaching note**: Validate the gap. Don't minimize it. Then map the path to close it.

---

### TYPE 3: COURAGE BLOCK
**Definition**: The client knows what they want AND has the capability, but
will not act. This is the most common presenting block.
**Presenting signs**:
- "I know what I need to do, but..."
- Extensive rationalization for delay
- Has been "almost ready" for a long time
- Makes plans but doesn't execute them
- Frequently references what could go wrong
- Readiness score stays below 7 even after insight
**Subtypes of courage blocks**:

| Subtype | Signal | Distinguishing question |
|---|---|---|
| Fear of failure | "What if it doesn't work?" | "What's the worst realistic outcome — and could you survive it?" |
| Fear of success | Sabotages near the finish line | "What changes in your life if this actually works?" |
| Fear of judgment | "What will people think?" | "Whose voice do you hear most when you imagine failing?" |
| Fear of loss | Knows what they must give up | "What would you have to let go of to move forward?" |
| Fear of commitment | Keeps options open | "What does keeping all options open actually cost you?" |

**Primary intervention**: Fear Inventory, courage wisdom (Gita Ch.2, Tao Wu Wei),
commitment question, scenario-testing
**Coaching note**: Do NOT push past fear without naming it. Naming it specifically
(not "fear" — but "fear of your father's judgment" or "fear of losing the identity
of someone who hasn't tried") is often itself the intervention.

---

### TYPE 4: CIRCUMSTANCE BLOCK
**Definition**: The client faces genuine external constraints that limit their options.
Not every obstacle is a story — some are real.
**Presenting signs**:
- Concrete, verifiable external constraints (financial, legal, health, caregiving)
- Has already attempted to remove the constraint without success
- Describes limitations specifically, not vaguely
- Does not catastrophize — presents facts
**Critical distinction**: Most "circumstance blocks" presented in coaching contain
a significant perception component. Test: would 10 different people in identical
circumstances all face the same limitation? If not → partly perception.
**What circumstance blocks require**:
1. First: validation. Do not rush to reframe a real constraint.
   "That is a real limitation. I want to honor that before we go anywhere else."
2. Then: distinguish what is fixed from what is flexible within the constraint
3. Then: find leverage within the constraint (Chanakya's resource doctrine)
4. Never: suggest that mindset alone can dissolve a structural problem
**Primary intervention**: Constraint Map, Kintsugi philosophy, Tao yielding wisdom,
strategic resource mapping

---

### TYPE 5: IDENTITY BLOCK
**Definition**: The client's goal conflicts with how they understand themselves.
They believe the goal belongs to a kind of person they are not.
**Presenting signs**:
- "I'm not really someone who..."
- "People like me don't..."
- Describes others achieving the goal as somehow different from themselves
- Has capability and some clarity, but frames the goal as "not realistic for me"
- Imposter syndrome in specific contexts
- Cultural or family scripts running beneath the surface
**This is the deepest block** and typically underlies the others.
Many clarity and courage blocks are actually identity blocks in disguise.
Test: "If someone exactly like you, from your exact background, achieved this goal —
would you still believe it wasn't for you?"
**Primary intervention**: Aham Brahmasmi (Upanishads), Persona/Mask teaching,
Family script inventory, "When did you decide..." question sequence

**The identity diagnostic sequence**:
1. "When did you first decide that [goal] wasn't for someone like you?"
2. "Whose voice is that — yours, or someone who shaped you?"
3. "If that belief weren't true — what would change?"
4. "What identity would you need to take on to pursue this? Does that feel wrong — and why?"

## AXIS 2 — EMOTIONAL DRIVER

Always run Axis 2 alongside Axis 1. The block type tells you *what* is stuck.
The emotional driver tells you *why* it persists.

### DRIVER A: FEAR
Most common underlying driver. Specify the type (see courage block subtypes above).
Generic "fear" is not useful. Name it precisely.

### DRIVER B: GRIEF / UNPROCESSED LOSS
Often invisible. Shows up as: stuckness, low energy, "I don't know why I can't move,"
recurring sadness disconnected from the stated problem.
**Test**: "Has something significant ended recently — a role, a relationship,
an era of life, a version of your future?"
If yes → grief work before strategic work. Load `crisis-protocol.md` if acute.

### DRIVER C: VALUES CONFLICT
The client wants two things that are genuinely incompatible, or their stated goal
conflicts with their actual values.
**Test**: "If you achieved this goal perfectly — would any part of you feel wrong?
Which part, and why?"
**Signal**: Persistent ambivalence despite clarity about the goal.

### DRIVER D: SYSTEMIC / STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINT
The limitation is real and external. See Type 4 above.
Distinguish from perception-based constraint through specificity and verification.

### DRIVER E: IDENTITY FRAGMENTATION
The client is between identities — they've left one (or are leaving it) but
haven't yet built the next. This creates a particular kind of paralysis.
**Signal**: "I used to know who I was" / major life transition underway
**Intervention**: Transition-specific wisdom (Vedic Ashrama system, Kintsugi),
identity bridge exercise

### DRIVER F: RELATIONAL ANCHOR
The client's stuck state is maintained by a relationship — they are unconsciously
calibrating their choices to keep someone else comfortable, or secure an attachment.
**Signal**: Every time they move toward the goal, they mention someone else's reaction.
**Test**: "If the people closest to you had no opinion about this — what would you do?"

## AXIS 3 — READINESS & TIMING

Assess where the client is in their readiness cycle.

### READINESS STAGES

**Stage 1 — Pre-contemplation**: Not yet aware a change is needed or possible.
Coaching focus: awareness-building, not action-planning.
Don't prescribe solutions they're not ready to implement.

**Stage 2 — Contemplation**: Aware something needs to change, ambivalent about it.
Coaching focus: exploring both sides of the ambivalence without rushing to resolution.
"What would you gain? What would you lose?" — hold both.

**Stage 3 — Preparation**: Has decided to change, is figuring out how.
Coaching focus: concrete planning, first steps, support structures.
This is the right stage for action items and exercises.

**Stage 4 — Action**: Has begun making changes.
Coaching focus: accountability, course correction, sustaining momentum.
Don't re-open the "should I do this" question.

**Stage 5 — Maintenance**: Has made the change, working to sustain it.
Coaching focus: relapse prevention, identity integration, celebrating progress.

**Misalignment risk**: Coaching for Stage 3 (here's your plan) when the client
is at Stage 2 (still ambivalent) creates resistance. Match your intervention to
their actual stage.

## RE-EVALUATION CHECKPOINTS

Run a diagnostic update whenever ANY of the following occur:

### Checkpoint A — Emotional Shift
Client becomes unexpectedly emotional, angry, or withdrawn.
→ The real block has surfaced. Re-diagnose from the emotion, not the content.
→ "Something just shifted in you. What's there?"

### Checkpoint B — Topic Pivot
Client suddenly changes the subject or introduces a new problem.
→ Either: the new topic IS the real issue
→ Or: avoidance of what was getting too close
→ "I notice we've moved to [new topic]. Is this connected to what we were just
exploring — or is this more important right now?"

### Checkpoint C — Flatness / Disengagement
Answers become shorter, less invested, more generic.
→ Usually means: the coaching has drifted from what actually matters to the client.
→ Re-engage: "I want to check in — are we working on the thing that matters most?"

### Checkpoint D — Breakthrough Signal
Client has an unexpected insight, laughs at something that hits deep, goes quiet.
→ Stay with it. Don't rush to the next question.
→ "Stay with that for a moment. What's there?"

### Checkpoint E — Resistance to the Diagnosis
Client pushes back on how you've framed their challenge.
→ This is NOT failure. It is diagnostic gold.
→ "You're pushing back on that. What feels wrong about it?"
→ Let them re-diagnose themselves. Their correction IS the insight.

## DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION BANK

Organized by what you're trying to uncover:

**To find the real problem beneath the presenting problem**:
- "If this specific problem were solved tomorrow — what would remain?"
- "What's the problem behind this problem?"
- "What would you be working on if this wasn't in the way?"

**To distinguish fear from circumstance**:
- "If success were guaranteed — would you do it?"
- "What's the worst realistic outcome — not the worst imaginable, the worst realistic?"
- "Do you know anyone who succeeded from a similar starting point?"

**To surface identity blocks**:
- "When did you decide this wasn't for you?"
- "Who is the kind of person who does this — and are you like them?"
- "What would you have to believe about yourself to pursue this?"

**To find the emotional driver**:
- "If this situation had a feeling at its center — what would it be?"
- "When you imagine taking the first step, what do you notice in your body?"
- "What does this remind you of from your past?"

**To assess readiness**:
- "On a scale of 1–10, how ready are you?"
- "What would need to be true for that number to go up by 2 points?"
- "What's the cost of waiting another 6 months?"

**To re-engage a disengaged client**:
- "Are we working on the right thing right now?"
- "What would you rather be exploring?"
- "What do you most need from this conversation today?"
