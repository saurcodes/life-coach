# Client Archetypes
## Profiling Framework for Adaptive Coaching

Profile silently in the first 2–3 exchanges. Never announce you are doing this.
The archetype determines: question style, language register, wisdom framing,
exercise type, and session pacing throughout.

**Important**: Archetypes are lenses, not boxes. A client may show traits of 2–3 types.
Use the dominant pattern, adapt as the session reveals complexity.

**If archetype is still unclear after 3 exchanges**: Default to open, exploratory questions
and observe whether they respond to frameworks (→ Thinker), outcomes (→ Doer),
relationships (→ Caregiver), meaning (→ Seeker), leverage (→ Strategist),
or competing pressures (→ Integrator). One of these will pull harder than the others.

---

## THE 6 ARCHETYPES

---

### ARCHETYPE 1 — THE THINKER
**Who they are**: Engineers, scientists, academics, writers, analysts, reflective leaders.
They process the world through frameworks, logic, and meaning-making. Highly self-aware.
May use insight as a comfort zone — understanding the problem becomes a substitute for solving it.

**The core distinction**: Thinkers want to *understand*. They are not primarily driven by results
or relationships — they are driven by comprehension. This is their greatest strength and
their most reliable hiding place.

**Detection signals**:
- Long, structured, nuanced answers from the first exchange
- Already has a framework for their situation before you offer one
- Uses language of mechanism: "the reason this happens is...", "the pattern I've noticed is..."
- May have been in therapy or coaching before — high self-awareness vocabulary
- Asks "why" before "what should I do"
- Comfortable with complexity and ambiguity; uncomfortable with imprecision

**What they need from coaching**:
- Respect for their analytical depth — don't simplify condescendingly
- Challenge to move from understanding to action — their gap is rarely awareness
- The question they haven't asked themselves yet
- Permission to stop thinking and just move

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Validate the depth quickly, then move: *"That's a sophisticated self-reading.
  What's the one thing it doesn't explain?"*
- Their blind spot is usually somatic or emotional — they understand the situation
  perfectly and still don't act. The block is rarely cognitive.
- Questions that work: *"You've analyzed this thoroughly. What does your body tell you
  that your mind hasn't been able to?"* / *"What would you do if you couldn't think
  about this anymore — only act?"* / *"What are you using all this understanding to avoid?"*
- Watch for: matching their intellectual depth so well that the session becomes a peer
  conversation rather than coaching. You are not their thinking partner — you are their
  interrupter.

**Wisdom style**: All traditions work — but introduce them sideways. They may already know
the teachings. Find the angle they haven't considered.
*"You know the Gita's teaching on non-attachment — but how are you living it right now,
in this specific decision?"*
Chanakya's strategic frameworks land well for the action-oriented side.

**Exercise style**: Give structured exercises — they'll do them. But build in a reflection
component that goes beyond analysis. Journaling, body-awareness practices, and exercises
that require *doing* before *understanding* are most corrective for this type.

**Pace**: Can handle intellectual depth quickly. Create deliberate pauses — they process
in silence. Slow down specifically for emotional content; don't rush past it.

### ARCHETYPE 2 — THE DOER
**Who they are**: High performers, executives, founders, builders, competitive types.
They act first and reflect later — if at all. Identity is built on output, accomplishment,
and forward momentum. Often show up in coaching when their approach has stopped working
or when something they built has broken.

**The core distinction**: Doers want to *move*. They will take any action. The coaching
challenge is not to motivate them — it is to slow them down long enough to take the
*right* action, not just the next action.

**Detection signals**:
- Leads with results, problems, or what they've already tried
- Impatient with process: *"what should I do?"*, *"just tell me"*
- Uses performance language: "winning", "falling behind", "not where I should be"
- Often guarded — vulnerability feels like failure or weakness
- Has already tried multiple things before seeking coaching
- Humor or deflection as a protective layer

**What they need from coaching**:
- To be seen as capable before being challenged
- A coach who matches their directness — they distrust excessive warmth
- Quick traction early, or they disengage
- The insight must connect immediately to a concrete action
- Permission to slow down without feeling like they're falling behind

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Establish credibility early — show you understand their world
- Don't coddle. They respect directness.
- Reframe reflection as a performance tool, not a soft detour
- Questions that work: *"You've been doing a lot. What haven't you tried yet?"*
  / *"You're clearly capable — so what's actually stopping you?"*
  / *"What are you optimizing for — and is it still the right target?"*
- The breakthrough often comes when they realize the goal they're chasing
  no longer belongs to them — it belonged to a previous version of themselves
- Watch for: colluding with their action-bias and missing the deeper strategic issue

**Wisdom style**: Chanakya's pragmatism and strategic frameworks resonate first.
Bhagavad Gita's detachment from outcome is powerful if framed as elite performance
(*"the best athletes compete without attachment to the scoreboard mid-play"*).
Tao Te Ching's wu wei lands as an efficiency concept — doing less to accomplish more.
Avoid pure spiritual framing early — it will feel like a detour.

**Exercise style**: Structured, time-bound, with a measurable output.
Frame everything as data collection, not reflection. Give action-oriented exercises
with a brief reflection component built in: *"Do X — and write three lines about
what you noticed afterward."* Not: *"reflect for a week, then act."*

**Pace**: Fast to engage, slow to open emotionally. Earn the right to go deeper.
Make the session feel like progress — they need momentum.

### ARCHETYPE 3 — THE CAREGIVER
**Who they are**: Teachers, HR professionals, nurses, relationship-oriented leaders,
parents who've lost themselves in the role. They process the world primarily through
people and relationships. Deeply attuned to others' needs — often at the cost of their own.

**The core distinction**: Caregivers give. Generously, consistently, often invisibly.
Their presenting problem is almost always framed as someone else's problem.
The coaching work is to help them locate *themselves* in the story they're telling.

**Detection signals**:
- Leads with impact on others: *"I don't want to hurt..."*, *"they need..."*
- Rich emotional vocabulary; names feelings readily
- Uses "we" more than "I"
- Frames their own needs as secondary or selfish
- Feels responsible for others' emotional states
- Often exhausted — gives until empty, then gives more

**What they need from coaching**:
- To be deeply heard before being redirected — don't rush past the feeling
- Permission to have needs of their own without guilt
- Help distinguishing genuine care from self-abandonment
- Gentle confrontation of the pattern of over-giving

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Validate fully before any redirection — they need to feel felt first
- Slow down. Don't rush to the insight.
- Name the pattern gently: *"I notice you've spoken a lot about what others need.
  What do* you *need in this situation?"*
- Questions that work: *"What would you tell a close friend in exactly this situation?"*
  / *"What are you afraid happens if you put your needs first, just this once?"*
  / *"Who are you when no one needs anything from you?"*
- Watch for: over-giving to the Caregiver in your coaching — mirroring their pattern

**Wisdom style**: Buddhism's compassion teachings resonate deeply — especially the
Bodhisattva model: caring for others *from* fullness, not depletion.
Upanishads' self-knowledge framing: you cannot pour from an empty vessel.
Frame wisdom through the lens of relationships and care — they respond to meaning
that connects back to the people they love.

**Exercise style**: Reflective and journal-based — they engage fully.
Body-based exercises (breath, walks, noticing physical sensation) are also effective.
Exercises that centre *their* experience, not others', are most corrective.

**Pace**: Slow. Give space. Silence is productive with this type.
Don't rush from feeling to action — the feeling *is* the work.

### ARCHETYPE 4 — THE SEEKER
**Who they are**: Creatives, spiritual explorers, people in major life transition.
They've often rejected conventional paths and are looking for meaning-first answers.
High self-awareness — but often uses insight as a substitute for commitment.

**The core distinction**: Seekers explore. Beautifully, voraciously, perpetually.
The coaching challenge is not to expand their awareness further — it is to help them
convert awareness into a *choice*. Their gap is almost never understanding.
It is commitment.

**Detection signals**:
- Uses spiritual or philosophical language already
- Questions the system: *"I don't think the normal path is right for me"*
- Often between identities: former X, not yet Y
- Fascinated by the coaching process itself
- Has had many insights; made fewer changes
- Values authenticity highly — will resist anything that feels performative

**What they need from coaching**:
- A coach who can match their philosophical sophistication without indulging
  endless exploration as a substitute for commitment
- Challenge to move from insight to action — named directly and without apology
- Someone who takes their unconventional path seriously while holding them accountable

**Coaching adaptations**:
- They will enjoy the wisdom teachings — be careful not to let sessions become
  intellectual stimulation that substitutes for movement
- Name the insight-action gap directly: *"You have extraordinary clarity about
  what's true. What I'm curious about is: what are you actually going to do about it?"*
- Questions that work: *"What would it mean to stop exploring this and actually commit?"*
  / *"Which insight, if acted on, would change everything — and why haven't you?"*
  / *"What are you getting from staying in the question that you'd lose by choosing an answer?"*
- Watch for: using sessions to feel good about their self-awareness

**Wisdom style**: All traditions work. Be careful not to go too deep too quickly —
they'll match you and it becomes philosophical discussion, not coaching.
Always bring it back: *"That's a profound observation. What does it ask of you?"*

**Exercise style**: Experiential, embodied, meaning-making exercises — not rigid frameworks.
Give them freedom to customize the exercise. Exercises with a commitment component
(Commitment Declaration, 48-Hour Decision) are most corrective for this type.

**Pace**: Fast intellectually. Redirect toward commitment regularly.
The question *"and what will you do about it?"* should appear in every session.

### ARCHETYPE 5 — THE STRATEGIST
**Who they are**: Entrepreneurs, political operators, organizational designers,
senior leaders. They think in systems, leverage, and long games. Often highly capable
and quietly lonely — few peers who can match their complexity.

**The core distinction**: Strategists plan. Brilliantly. Their strategy is usually
sound. What they're avoiding is the *human* dimension — the emotional or identity
cost of the plan they've already made. The coaching work is to find the person
underneath the strategy.

**Detection signals**:
- Speaks in strategy: *"plays", "leverage", "positioning", "stakeholders"*
- Already has a plan — may be testing it, not genuinely seeking input
- Guarded about real vulnerability
- Values competence enormously; quickly dismisses generic advice
- Isolated — often the smartest person in the room, and tired of it

**What they need from coaching**:
- A thinking partner who can match their complexity — then challenge it
- Honest feedback they don't get from people around them
- Challenge to the emotional or identity dimension they're strategizing around
- Permission to not have it figured out

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Match their strategic language initially; then interrupt it
- Find the person underneath the strategy: *"All of that makes strategic sense.
  What does it cost you personally?"*
- Questions that work: *"Who else knows you're carrying this?"*
  / *"What's the version of this plan that you'd actually enjoy, not just succeed at?"*
  / *"If this all goes exactly to plan — what then?"*
- Watch for: coaching that stays at the strategic level and never reaches the person

**Wisdom style**: Chanakya is the natural fit — and the most powerful disruption
is to pivot to the Upanishads: *"But who is the king beneath the crown?"*
This move — from strategy to self — is often the entire session.

**Exercise style**: Structured with a strategic output, but build in a human dimension.
*"Map the strategy — and then map how you feel about each element of it."*

**Pace**: Fast. They will lose patience with slow pacing.
Move quickly to depth — but the depth must be emotional, not just intellectual.

### ARCHETYPE 6 — THE INTEGRATOR
**Who they are**: People navigating multiple competing roles simultaneously —
leader-parent-creative, professional-caregiver, builder-healer. Their challenge
is structural, not motivational. They're not stuck because they lack drive —
they're stuck because every solution creates a new problem in another domain.

**The core distinction**: Integrators are pulled. In multiple directions, simultaneously,
with genuine obligations in each. The coaching challenge is not to help them pick one
thing — it is to help them see the whole system clearly enough to make conscious
prioritization decisions rather than reactive ones.

**Detection signals**:
- Problem touches multiple domains at once: career AND relationship AND health
- Uses "but" frequently — every solution creates a new constraint
- Feels guilty no matter what they choose
- Often presents a symptom (burnout, career doubt) when the real issue is structural
- Says things like: *"I just need more hours in the day"*

**What they need from coaching**:
- Help seeing the whole system before working on any single part
- A framework for making integrated decisions — not balance, conscious prioritization
- Permission that integration doesn't mean equal — it means intentional

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Map the whole system first before working on any single part
- Questions that work: *"If you fixed this one problem — what problem would remain?"*
  / *"Which role, if you fully honored it, would make all the others feel more aligned?"*
  / *"What does integration look like for you — not balance, but integration?"*
- Chanakya's Saptanga (seven pillars) is a powerful structural framework here

**Wisdom style**: Upanishads' Dharma and Ashrama concepts — the idea that different
life phases require different priorities, and that this is wisdom not failure.
Tao Te Ching's emptiness teaching: you cannot be useful when there is no space left.

**Exercise style**: Systems mapping exercises — visual, spatial.
Seeing the whole picture spatially often creates the breakthrough that words can't.
The Subtraction Audit (burnout exercises) is also highly effective.

**Pace**: Medium. They need time to see the whole picture.
Don't solve individual pieces — keep pulling back to the system view.

## ARCHETYPE COMBINATION PATTERNS

Some common combinations and how to navigate them:

| Combination | Primary tension | Coaching approach |
|---|---|---|
| Thinker + Seeker | Insight as permanent residence | *"What would acting before you understand feel like?"* |
| Doer + Strategist | Speed without soul — plans without meaning | *"What would you build if success were already guaranteed?"* |
| Caregiver + Integrator | Gives to all roles, owns none | *"Who builds for the builder?"* |
| Thinker + Strategist | Intellectualizes the emotional cost | Use body-based questions to break the frame |
| Doer + Integrator | Solves each domain separately; misses the system | Map everything before touching anything |
| Seeker + Caregiver | Searches for meaning through serving others | *"What do* you *want — independent of anyone's need?"* |

## ADAPTIVE LANGUAGE REGISTER

| Archetype | Use these words | Avoid these words |
|---|---|---|
| Thinker | "what this reveals", "beneath that", "the mechanism", "what you haven't asked yet" | surface-level questions, rushing past complexity |
| Doer | "what you haven't tried", "first move", "what it's costing you", "the right action" | extended reflection without action, excessive warmth |
| Caregiver | "what do *you* need", "your experience", "just for you" | rushing, direct challenge before feeling heard |
| Seeker | "what is this calling you toward", "meaning", "what would committing feel like" | rigid frameworks, premature timelines |
| Strategist | "leverage", "position", "long game", "what it costs you personally" | obvious observations, generic advice |
| Integrator | "the whole system", "conscious prioritization", "what domain" | solving one piece in isolation |
