# Client Archetypes
## Profiling Framework for Adaptive Coaching

Profile silently in the first 2–3 exchanges. Never announce you are doing this.
The archetype determines: question style, language register, wisdom framing,
exercise type, and session pacing throughout.

**Important**: Archetypes are lenses, not boxes. A client may show traits of 2–3 types.
Use the dominant pattern, adapt as the session reveals complexity.

---

## THE 8 ARCHETYPES

---

### ARCHETYPE 1 — THE ANALYST
**Who they are**: Engineers, scientists, finance professionals, systems thinkers.
They process the world through logic, data, frameworks, and causal reasoning.

**Detection signals**:
- Uses quantitative language: "ROI", "metrics", "efficiency", "optimize", "data"
- Asks "why" and "how" before "what should I feel"
- Tends toward long, structured sentences
- May intellectualize emotions ("logically I know I should feel X but...")
- Skeptical of "soft" concepts unless grounded in mechanism

**What they need from coaching**:
- Frameworks before feelings
- Evidence before exhortation
- Respect for their analytical capability — don't simplify condescendingly
- Permission to acknowledge emotion as data, not weakness

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Lead with structured frameworks (the 4-problem diagnostic works well)
- Reframe feelings as signals/data, not threats to logic
- Use wisdom that has internal mechanism: Chanakya's strategic models,
  the Tao's paradoxes framed as systems insights
- Questions that work: "What does the evidence actually support here?"
  "If you ran this as an experiment — what would the hypothesis be?"
  "What assumption is load-bearing in your current model?"
- Avoid: vague spiritual language without grounding, excessive empathy before
  they've processed intellectually

**Wisdom style**: Frame ancient teachings as systems and principles, not stories.
"Chanakya's four-lever model" lands better than "there's a parable about..."

**Exercise style**: Give structured exercises with defined inputs/outputs.
They will do the exercise — they value the data it produces.

**Pace**: Can handle intellectual depth quickly. Don't slow-walk the logic.
Slow down only for emotional content.

### ARCHETYPE 2 — THE ACHIEVER
**Who they are**: High performers, executives, competitive types.
Identity built on accomplishment, progress, and being seen as capable.

**Detection signals**:
- Leads with accomplishments before the problem
- Uses language of performance: "winning", "falling behind", "not where I should be"
- Often guarded — vulnerability feels like failure
- May present problem as tactical when it's actually identity-level
- Short answers initially; opens up once trust is established
- Humor or deflection as protective layer

**What they need from coaching**:
- To be seen as capable before being challenged
- A coach who matches their directness — they distrust excessive warmth
- Permission to acknowledge that winning isn't always the answer
- Questions that honor their intelligence while exposing the blind spot

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Establish credibility early — show you understand their world
- Don't coddle. They respect directness.
- Reframe vulnerability as strategic intelligence, not weakness
- Questions that work: "You're clearly capable — so what's actually stopping you?"
  "What would the version of you from 5 years ago say about this choice?"
  "What are you optimizing for — and is it still the right target?"
- The breakthrough often comes when they realize the goal they're chasing
  is no longer theirs — it belonged to a previous version of themselves

**Wisdom style**: Chanakya's strategic frameworks resonate most.
Bhagavad Gita's detachment from outcome is powerful if framed as elite performance
("the best athletes compete without attachment to the scoreboard mid-play").
Avoid Tao Te Ching early — "doing nothing" will feel like failure to them.

**Exercise style**: Structured, time-bound, with a measurable output.
Loose exercises will be dismissed. Frame as "data collection" not "reflection."

**Pace**: Fast to engage intellectually, slow to open emotionally.
Earn the right to go deeper.

---

### ARCHETYPE 3 — THE EMPATH
**Who they are**: Caregivers, teachers, HR professionals, relationship-oriented leaders.
They process the world primarily through people and emotions.

**Detection signals**:
- Leads with impact on others: "I don't want to hurt...", "they need..."
- Rich emotional vocabulary; names feelings readily
- Often puts their own needs last
- May frame their problem as someone else's problem
- Uses "we" more than "I"
- Feels responsibility for others' emotional states

**What they need from coaching**:
- To be deeply heard before being redirected
- Permission to have needs of their own
- Help distinguishing between genuine care and self-abandonment
- Gentle confrontation of patterns of over-giving

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Validate fully before any redirection — they need to feel felt
- Slow down. Don't rush to the insight.
- Name the pattern gently: "I notice you've spoken a lot about what others need.
  What do *you* need in this situation?"
- Questions that work: "What would you tell a close friend in exactly this situation?"
  "What are you afraid happens if you put your needs first, just this once?"
  "Who are you when no one needs anything from you?"
- Watch for: coaching that mirrors their pattern (over-giving to the client)

**Wisdom style**: Buddhism (compassion, impermanence) resonates deeply.
The Bodhisattva model — caring for others *from* fullness, not depletion —
is transformative for this type.
Upanishads' self-knowledge framing: "You cannot pour from an empty vessel."

**Exercise style**: Reflective, journal-based exercises. They will engage fully.
Body-based exercises (breath, walks, noticing physical sensation) are also effective.

**Pace**: Slow. Give space. Silence is productive with this type.

---

### ARCHETYPE 4 — THE SEEKER
**Who they are**: Creatives, spiritual explorers, people in major life transition.
They've often rejected conventional paths and are looking for meaning-first answers.

**Detection signals**:
- Uses spiritual or philosophical language already
- Questions the system: "I don't think the normal path is right for me"
- Often in-between identities: former X, not yet Y
- High self-awareness but sometimes uses insight as avoidance of action
- May be fascinated by the coaching process itself

**What they need from coaching**:
- A coach who can match their philosophical sophistication without indulging
  endless exploration as a substitute for commitment
- Challenge to move from insight to action — their gap is rarely awareness
- Someone who takes their unconventional path seriously while holding them
  accountable to results

**Coaching adaptations**:
- They will enjoy the wisdom teachings — be careful not to let this become
  intellectual stimulation that substitutes for movement
- Name the insight-action gap directly: "You have extraordinary clarity about
  what's true. What I'm curious about is: what are you actually going to do about it?"
- Questions that work: "What would it mean to stop exploring this and actually commit?"
  "Which insight, if acted on, would change everything — and why haven't you?"
  "What are you getting from staying in the question that you'd lose by choosing an answer?"
- Watch for: using sessions to feel good about their self-awareness

**Wisdom style**: All traditions work. Be careful not to go too deep too quickly —
they'll match you and it becomes a philosophical discussion, not coaching.
Bring it back: "That's a profound observation. What does it ask of you?"

**Exercise style**: Experiential, embodied, meaning-making exercises. Not rigid frameworks.
Give them the freedom to customize the exercise — they'll engage more.

**Pace**: Fast intellectually, but needs to be redirected toward commitment regularly.

### ARCHETYPE 5 — THE STRATEGIST
**Who they are**: Entrepreneurs, political operators, organizational designers.
They think in systems, leverage, and long games. Often highly capable and quite lonely.

**Detection signals**:
- Speaks in strategy: "plays", "leverage", "positioning", "stakeholders"
- Already has a plan — may be testing it, not genuinely seeking input
- Can be guarded about real vulnerability
- Often isolated — few peers who can match their thinking
- Values competence enormously; dismisses what they perceive as generic advice

**What they need from coaching**:
- A thinking partner who can match their complexity
- Challenge to the *emotional* or *identity* dimension they're avoiding
- Honest feedback — they are often surrounded by people who won't challenge them
- Permission to not have it figured out

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Match their strategic language initially; then gently interrupt it
- Find the human underneath the strategy: "All of that makes strategic sense.
  What does it cost you personally?"
- Questions that work: "Who else knows you're carrying this?"
  "What's the version of this plan that you'd actually enjoy, not just succeed at?"
  "If this all goes exactly to plan — what then?"
- Watch for: coaching that stays at the strategic level and never reaches the person

**Wisdom style**: Chanakya is the natural fit — and the most powerful disruption
is to then pivot to the Upanishads' question: "But who is the king beneath the crown?"

**Exercise style**: Structured with a strategic output, but build in a reflection component.
"Map the strategy — and then map how you feel about each element of it."

**Pace**: Fast. They will lose patience with slow pacing. Move quickly, go deep fast.

### ARCHETYPE 6 — THE PHILOSOPHER
**Who they are**: Deep thinkers, academics, writers, reflective leaders.
Already highly self-aware; process through meaning-making and narrative.

**Detection signals**:
- Long, nuanced answers from the first exchange
- Already using frameworks to understand their situation
- References books, thinkers, personal history
- May have been in therapy or coaching before
- Comfortable with complexity and ambiguity

**What they need from coaching**:
- A coach who can honor their depth without getting lost in it
- Challenge to act, not just understand
- Someone who asks the question they haven't asked themselves

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Validate the depth quickly and move: "That's a sophisticated self-reading.
  What's the one thing it doesn't explain?"
- Their blind spot is usually emotional or somatic — they understand their situation
  perfectly and still don't act. The block is rarely cognitive.
- Questions that work: "You've analyzed this thoroughly. What does your body tell you
  that your mind hasn't been able to?"
  "What would you do if you couldn't think about this anymore — only act?"
  "What are you using all this understanding to avoid?"
- Watch for: matching their philosophical depth so well that the session becomes
  a conversation between equals rather than a coaching relationship

**Wisdom style**: All traditions work — but introduce them sideways.
They may already know the teachings. Find the angle they haven't considered.
"You know Anicca — but how are you living it *right now* in this decision?"

**Exercise style**: Journaling, dialogue exercises, somatic awareness practices.
They need embodiment, not more thinking.

**Pace**: Variable. Can go deep fast intellectually. Create pauses — they process
in the silence.

---

### ARCHETYPE 7 — THE INTEGRATOR
**Who they are**: People navigating multiple competing roles simultaneously —
leader-parent-creative, professional-caregiver, builder-healer. Their challenge
is integration, not any single domain.

**Detection signals**:
- Problem touches multiple domains at once: career AND relationship AND health
- Uses "but" frequently — every solution creates a new problem in another area
- Feels pulled in multiple directions and guilty about each one
- May present a symptom (burnout, career doubt) when the real issue is structural

**What they need from coaching**:
- Help seeing the whole system, not just the presenting part
- A framework for making integrated decisions
- Permission that integration doesn't mean equal — it means conscious prioritization

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Map the whole system first before working on any single part
- Questions that work: "If you fixed this one problem — what problem would remain?"
  "Which role, if you fully honored it, would make all the others feel more aligned?"
  "What does integration look like for you — not balance, but integration?"
- Chanakya's Saptanga (seven pillars) is the perfect structural framework here

**Wisdom style**: Upanishads' Dharma concept — different actions required at different
life stages (*Ashrama* — student, householder, forest dweller, sage). The idea that
the right action shifts across life phases is deeply validating.

**Exercise style**: Systems mapping exercises. Visual. The Constraint Map or
a Life Domains Wheel — seeing it spatially often creates breakthrough.

**Pace**: Medium. They need time to see the whole picture.

---

### ARCHETYPE 8 — THE BUILDER
**Who they are**: Founders, makers, doers. They act fast, build first, and find
reflection uncomfortable. Often show up in coaching when their approach has stopped working.

**Detection signals**:
- Impatient with process: "what should I do?"
- Has already tried multiple things before seeking coaching
- Speaks in outcomes and results
- Uncomfortable sitting with uncertainty
- May be in crisis — the thing they built broke, or they burnt out building it

**What they need from coaching**:
- Quick wins early — or they disengage
- The insight must be attached to a concrete action immediately
- Permission to slow down without feeling like they're falling behind

**Coaching adaptations**:
- Get to action language quickly — but make sure it's the right action
- The coaching challenge: they'll take any action. Help them take the *right* action.
- Questions that work: "You've been doing a lot. What haven't you tried yet?"
  "What would you build if you knew it would fail — and build it anyway?"
  "What does success look like if speed is removed from the equation?"
- Watch for: colluding with their action-bias and missing the deeper strategic issue

**Wisdom style**: Chanakya's pragmatism first. Then introduce Tao's wu wei as
a productivity concept — "doing less to accomplish more" frames as efficiency,
not passivity.

**Exercise style**: Action-oriented with a reflection component built in.
"Do X — and write three lines about what you noticed afterward."
Not: "reflect for a week and then act."

**Pace**: Fast. They need momentum. Make the session feel like progress.

## ARCHETYPE COMBINATION PATTERNS

Some common combinations and how to navigate them:

| Combination | Primary tension | Coaching approach |
|---|---|---|
| Analyst + Achiever | Logic justifies avoidance of emotion | Use data to surface the emotional cost |
| Empath + Builder | Builds for others, neglects self | "Who builds for the builder?" |
| Seeker + Philosopher | Insight as avoidance of commitment | "What would choosing actually feel like?" |
| Strategist + Integrator | Plans everything except their inner life | Map the strategy AND the person |
| Achiever + Builder | Speed and success as identity | Slow down to find what they're running from |

## ADAPTIVE LANGUAGE REGISTER

| Archetype | Use these words | Avoid these words |
|---|---|---|
| Analyst | "data", "framework", "signal", "mechanism" | "feel into", "energy", "universe" |
| Achiever | "edge", "what's costing you", "next level" | "it's okay to fail", "be gentle" |
| Empath | "what do *you* need", "your experience" | rushing, direct challenge too early |
| Seeker | "what is this calling you toward", "meaning" | rigid frameworks, timelines too early |
| Strategist | "leverage", "position", "long game" | obvious observations, generic advice |
| Philosopher | "what this reveals", "beneath that" | surface-level questions |
| Integrator | "the whole system", "which domain" | solving one piece in isolation |
| Builder | "what you haven't tried", "first move" | extended reflection without action |
